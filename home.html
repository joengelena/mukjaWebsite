K-Fried Rice

Kimchi fried rice, known as "Kimchi Bokkeumbap" in Korean, is a popular and flavorful dish that incorporates kimchi, a traditional Korean fermented cabbage dish.

Description

Kimchi fried rice is made by stir-frying cooked rice with kimchi and a variety of ingredients to create a delicious and satisfying dish. The process usually involves chopping or shredding kimchi and mixing it with rice in a hot pan. Other common additions include vegetables such as onions, carrots, and corn, as well as protein sources like diced meat (such as pork, beef, or chicken) or tofu. The dish is often seasoned with soy sauce, sesame oil, and gochugaru (Korean red pepper flakes) for added spice. It's a quick and easy way to turn leftover rice and kimchi into a tasty one-pan meal that showcases the bold and tangy flavours of Korean cuisine.

Triangle Kimbap

Kimbap is made with steamed rice and various ingredients such as meat and vegetables rolled with dried seaweed called "kim" in Korean. As a simple-easy and cheap snack to go, Korea sells Triangle Kimbap in convinient stores.

Description

The major difference between normal kimbap and triangle kimbap is the shape. The primary distinction between regular kimbap and triangle kimbap lies in their shapes. Traditional kimbap features a cylindrical roll composed of fresh rice seasoned with salt and sesame oil, complemented by an assortment of vegetables like spinach, carrot, egg, pickled radish, ham, and more. In contrast, triangle kimbap, commonly found in Korean convenience stores, is pre-packaged and stored in refrigerators, eliminating the freshly made aspect. Triangle kimbap is different from regular kimbap mainly in its shape and filling. While regular kimbap is a rolled mix of fresh rice, veggies, and more, triangle kimbap is pre-made and stored in the fridge due to its convenience store origin. It's wrapped in seaweed but takes on a triangular shape. Unlike regular kimbap, which can have a mix of ingredients, triangle kimbap often focuses on one main flavour like bulgogi, spicy pork, or tuna mayo.

Mandu

Korean dumplings, known as "mandu," are delightful parcels of flavor that hold a special place in Korean cuisine. These dumplings come in various shapes and sizes, with popular types including steamed, boiled, or pan-fried mandu. The thin, delicate wrappers are typically made from a mixture of wheat flour and water, creating a tender texture that encases a delectable filling.

Description

The fillings for Korean dumplings are diverse and can include a combination of minced meat, vegetables, tofu, or kimchi, seasoned with a blend of soy sauce, garlic, sesame oil, and other aromatic spices. Common choices for the filling are pork, beef, or a vegetarian mix, offering a wide range of options to suit different preferences. It also holds cultural significance as a dish often made during family gatherings. Families come together to prepare the savoury dumplings, turning the process into a communal and bonding activity. Crafting mandu becomes a shared tradition, creating not only delicious dumplings but also cherished memories during these family cooking sessions.

K-Corndog

In Korea, corndogs are a common and easy-to-find snack. People often add them as a topping to tteokbokki, along with cheese. Since tteokbokki has a bit of a spicy and sweet taste, the crispy corndog helps balance out the flavors!
